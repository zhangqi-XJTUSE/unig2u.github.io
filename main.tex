\begin{abstract}
Unified multimodal models have recently demonstrated strong generative capabilities, yet whether and when generation improves understanding remains unclear. We introduce \textsc{UniG2U-Bench}, a comprehensive benchmark for studying generation-to-understanding (G2U) across seven reasoning regimes that require varying degrees of implicit or explicit visual transformation.
Extensive experiments on over 30 models reveal that generation does not yield universal gains: on most tasks, unified models underperform their corresponding base VLMs, and Generate-then-Answer(GtA) inference typically degrades performance relative to Direct inference. However, structured improvements consistently emerge in spatial and illusion-sensitive subtasks, where transformation-aware representations are beneficial. Moreover, G2U gain patterns are not random: tasks with similar reasoning structures and models sharing the same architecture or (more strongly) the same base model exhibit correlated behaviors, suggesting that generation–understanding coupling induces class-consistent inductive biases rather than isolated empirical fluctuations.
\end{abstract}

Recent advances in multimodal foundation models have led to a growing interest in unifying understanding and generation within a single architecture~\cite{yang2025survey}. Unified models have demonstrated impressive performance in image synthesis, editing, and cross-modal generation, suggesting that strong perceptual and semantic understanding serves as a critical foundation for high-quality generation~\cite{zou2025unimmmu,xie2025mmeunify,li2025unieval}.

Despite this progress, the interaction between understanding and generation has been studied predominantly in a single direction: understanding improves generation. Whether generation itself can enhance understanding by serving as an external medium for reasoning, verification, or hypothesis construction, remains largely unexplored. 

A key reason is the absence of benchmarks that explicitly evaluate understanding \emph{through} generation. Most existing benchmarks for multimodal understanding emphasize recognition, classification, or verbal reasoning over static visual inputs. As a result, strong performance can often be achieved by first converting images into dense textual descriptions and then applying a text-only large language model~\cite{xing2025caprl,qiao2024prism}. Consequently, many questions in popular understanding benchmarks can be answered without relying on visual information that is inherently non-linguistic. However, in many complex scenarios—such as drawing auxiliary constructions in geometry, reconstructing spatial layouts, or externalizing intermediate states in puzzles and games—effective understanding cannot be reduced to linguistic abstraction alone. In such cases, generation must serve as an internal or external reasoning mechanism that enables access to visual transformations not captured by textual descriptions.

The rapid evolution of unified multimodal models—such as Bagel, OmniGen, Show-o, and Janus—has proven that generation and understanding can coexist within a shared parameter space. While recent benchmarks like UniMMU~\cite{zou2025unimmmu} and MME-Unify~\cite{xie2025mmeunify} evaluate these models, they primarily assess understanding and generation in isolation (i.e., asking ``can it answer?'' and ``can it draw?'' independently). They fail to address whether the generative capacity actively \emph{aids} the understanding process. Furthermore, evaluating these models is challenging due to the confounding variables of model scaling and backbone differences. To systematically study this, we introduce \textbf{UniG2U}, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate when and how generation helps understanding. Unlike previous benchmarks, UniG2U strictly pairs cutting-edge unified models with their purely discriminative base VLMs under matched inference protocols. By doing so, we cleanly isolate the Generation-to-Understanding (G2U) capability, allowing us to accurately assess whether explicit or implicit visual generation facilitates multimodal reasoning.

We benchmarked over 30 models across seven task categories (see Fig.~\ref{fig:task-selection-coverage}). By rigorously comparing unified models with their base VLMs under both direct and Generate-then-Answer  inference protocols, our analysis reveals a nuanced Generation-to-Understanding (G2U) dynamic. First, simply integrating generative capabilities incurs an overall performance degradation on standard understanding tasks due to inherent objective interference. However, this unification yields distinct capability improvements on tasks requiring explicit visual simulation, such as Spatial Intelligence. Second, we find that the Generate-then-Answer  reasoning paradigm—generating intermediate images prior to answering—is a double-edged sword. While it significantly aids comprehension in transformation-intensive scenarios where intermediate states can be explicitly grounded, it severely propagates visual errors in structurally constrained, logic-intensive tasks. Ultimately, our findings demonstrate that the effectiveness of generation strictly depends on its alignment fidelity, exhibiting highly predictable trends across models with shared base weights and tasks with similar cognitive demands.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A Novel Testbed:} We introduce UniG2U, the largest unified-model evaluation testbed to date designed specifically for the Generation-to-Understanding (G2U) paradigm. It covers 3,000 carefully curated instances across seven reasoning categories, providing standardized data, protocols, and scripts for reproducible evaluation.
    
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Experiments:} We conduct the largest large-scale study of unified models so far, benchmarking over 30 models across autoregressive, diffusion-based, and hybrid architectures. We systematically isolate G2U gains by strictly comparing unified models against their foundational VLM baselines.
    
    \item \textbf{Deep Mechanistic Insights:} Our analyses demystify when generation helps or harms understanding. We expose the inherent architectural trade-offs between generative and discriminative objectives, the vulnerabilities of the Generate-then-Answer  visual reasoning paradigm, and the critical role of intermediate alignment fidelity.
\end{enumerate}
\section{Related Works}



\subsection{Unified Multimodal Models}
Early multimodal systems largely followed a \emph{disjoint} design, coupling a vision--language understanding model with a separate image generator in a pipeline, which made end-to-end credit assignment across modalities difficult \citep{alayrac2022flamingo,li2023blip2}.
The rise of large multimodal LMs strengthened the trend toward a \emph{single interaction interface}---using language to control multimodal perception and reasoning---yet image generation often remained externalized as a separate module \citep{huang2023kosmos1}.
In parallel, diffusion-style text-to-image synthesis made ``understanding+generation'' pipelines practical at scale \citep{rombach2022ldm}.
More recently, research has pushed toward \emph{unified multimodal models} that can both \emph{understand} and \emph{generate} within one interface, motivated by shared representation learning and tighter coupling between perception and synthesis \citep{team2024chameleon,xie2024showo}.
Token-based early-fusion models suggest that a single next-token objective over mixed-modal sequences can support both behaviors \citep{team2024chameleon}, while hybrid formulations combine autoregressive decoding with discrete diffusion/flow-style generation inside a single transformer to broaden modality patterns \citep{xie2024showo}.
Scaling these designs toward broader modality coverage (e.g., image/video) has made unified interfaces increasingly competitive as general-purpose multimodal backbones \citep{xie2025showo2,deng2025bagel,chen2025januspro}.
Surveys have begun to systematize this design space and repeatedly emphasize that \emph{evaluation} remains a key bottleneck for understanding when unification yields \emph{genuine} capability gains \citep{yang2025survey,zhao2025unifiedsurvey}.

\subsection{Benchmarks for Unified Models}
Benchmarks have begun to operationalize ``unification'' in different ways, including capability coverage, mixed-modality output competence, and explicit bidirectional synergy protocols \citep{xie2025mmeunify,shi2025realunify,zou2025unimmmu,li2026ueval}.
MME-Unify (MME-U) provides a scalable audit via three domains (understanding, generation, and mixed/unified tasks) and makes unified tasks \emph{quantifiable} by casting them into \emph{text-option + image-option} selection, judging generated images through CLIP-style similarity \citep{xie2025mmeunify,radford2021clip}.
RealUnify defines unification as \emph{bidirectional synergy} (UEG/GEU) and introduces paired \emph{direct} and \emph{Generate-then-Answer } protocols to localize whether failures stem from core skills or integration \citep{shi2025realunify}.
Uni-MMMU centers multi-discipline reasoning with Gen$\rightarrow$Und / Und$\rightarrow$Gen paradigms and emphasizes verifiable intermediates with dual-channel (text+image) scoring \citep{zou2025unimmmu,yue2023mmmu}.
UEval evaluates real-world requests requiring both text and images with item-level rubrics and a runnable pipeline/leaderboard \citep{li2026ueval,li2025unieval}.

Despite these advances, existing benchmarks still fall short of diagnosing \emph{generation-to-understanding} (G2U) as a capability phenomenon: (i) what constitutes a \emph{G2U task} where intermediate visuals are intrinsically useful under matched budgets, and (ii) how results vary across fine-grained \emph{task regimes} and \emph{model archetypes}.
MME-U offers scale but does not isolate synergy or define G2U task structure \citep{xie2025mmeunify}; RealUnify and Uni-MMMU introduce synergy-aware protocols but cover a narrower slice of task types and unified-model designs \citep{shi2025realunify,zou2025unimmmu}; UEval focuses on mixed-output quality rather than causal G2U effects \citep{li2026ueval}.
UniG2U complements this landscape by curating G2U-centric regimes (e.g., spatial manipulation, state externalization, visual illusions), expanding model coverage across end-to-end, decoupled, and agentic designs, and analyzing intermediate-visual alignment and failure modes under controlled budgets.

\section{Formulations and Definitions}
\label{sec:formulation}

This section formalizes the core objects studied in UniG2U.
We (i) define \emph{unified multimodal models} (UMMs) and the capability-level notion of \emph{Generation Helps Understanding} (G2U),
(ii) introduce a taxonomy of UMMs used throughout the paper, and
(iii) distinguish \emph{implicit} vs.\ \emph{explicit} generation-augmented understanding under a unified evaluation.

\subsection{Definitions: UMMs and G2U}
\label{sec:unified_model_def}

\paragraph{Task setting.}
Each instance is $x=(V,T,Q)$ with answer $y\in\mathcal{Y}$.

\paragraph{Pure understanding models.}
A \emph{pure understanding} model maps the given input to an answer,
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}=M_U(x), \qquad M_U\in\mathcal{M}_U,
\end{equation}
and does not expose an interface for producing intermediate \emph{visual} artifacts intended to be re-consumed for the same instance.

\paragraph{Unified multimodal models (UMMs).}
A \emph{unified multimodal model} supports multimodal understanding together with a generative interface that can produce
a visual artifact $G$ (image, sketch, annotation, reconstruction, edit, \emph{etc.}) conditioned on multimodal input.
Conceptually, UMMs are motivated by the hypothesis that generation can (i) shape internal representations through unified training,
and/or (ii) act as a controllable reasoning resource via intermediate artifacts.

\paragraph{Defining ``Generation Helps Understanding'' (G2U).}
\label{sec:g2u_def}
Let $\mathcal{A}(\cdot,\cdot)$ be the task metric and define performance on dataset $\mathcal{D}$ as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Perf}(M;\mathcal{D})
= \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\mathcal{D}}\big[\mathcal{A}(M(x),y)\big].
\end{equation}
Let $\mathcal{B}$ denote the strongest \emph{pure understanding} baseline under the evaluation budget:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{B}
=\arg\max_{M\in\mathcal{M}_U}\ \mathrm{Perf}(M;\mathcal{D}).
\label{eq:best_understanding_baseline}
\end{equation}
For a unified system $M_{UM}$, the \textbf{G2U gain} is
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathrm{G2U}}(M_{UM};\mathcal{D})
= \mathrm{Perf}(M_{UM};\mathcal{D}) - \mathrm{Perf}(\mathcal{B};\mathcal{D}).
\end{equation}
We emphasize \textbf{capability comparability} (strong baselines) and \textbf{budget matching} so gains are not merely ``more compute.''

\subsection{Taxonomy of Unified Multimodal Models}
\label{sec:umm_taxonomy}

We group evaluated UMMs into three categories: \textbf{end-to-end unified models} (E2E),
\textbf{decoupled unified systems} (Decoupled), and \textbf{agentic unified models} (UM-Ag).
\begin{figure*}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{2.10 图 (2).pdf}
  \caption{Taxonomy of unified multimodal models (UMMs). All models annotated in the figure are benchmarked in this work.}
  \label{fig:umm-taxonomy}
\end{figure*}

All categories can be expressed via a shared factorization,
\begin{equation}
G=\mathrm{Gen}(x), \qquad \hat{y}=\mathrm{Ans}(x,G),
\label{eq:unified_factorization}
\end{equation}
where $G=\varnothing$ recovers direct answering. The taxonomy differs along two axes:
\emph{(i) where the unification lives} (shared parameters vs.\ modular orchestration),
and \emph{(ii) how $G$ is produced and consumed} (single-shot vs.\ iterative closed-loop).

\paragraph{(1) End-to-end unified models (E2E).}
E2E models are \emph{parameter-coupled}: $\mathrm{Gen}$ and $\mathrm{Ans}$ are realized by a single model with parameters $\theta$
. In practice, $G$ is produced \emph{within} the same forward process that yields
the final answer, so the intermediate artifact is often \emph{latent} (hidden states) or \emph{lightweight} (a short rationale),
and the system does not explicitly invoke external modules. A defining property is that improvements to generation and understanding
are obtained by end-to-end training or prompting of one unified model, rather than by swapping components.
Representative instances in our study include \emph{Bagel}, \emph{STAR-7B}, and
\emph{UniPic2-Metaquery-9B}, which operate under a unified single-model interface.

\paragraph{(2) Decoupled unified systems (Decoupled).}
Decoupled systems are \emph{module-separated}: $\mathrm{Gen}$ and $\mathrm{Ans}$ are implemented by different modules
(with parameters $\phi$ and $\psi$), so unification is operational rather than parametric.
Concretely, they expose an \emph{explicit} intermediate artifact $G$ , which is then consumed by an answering module:
\begin{equation}
G = \mathrm{Gen}_{\phi}(x), \qquad \hat{y} = \mathrm{Ans}_{\psi}(x, G).
\end{equation}
This modularity enables component-level ablations and substitutions while keeping $\mathrm{Ans}_\psi$ fixed.
Typical examples include \emph{Bagel two-stage} (with-gen), \emph{OneCAT} (with prompt-gen / with image-gen), and
\emph{TokenFlow-XL} (with prompt-gen), where $G$ is surfaced explicitly before answering.
However, the interaction is typically \emph{single-pass}: $G$ is generated once (or via a fixed pipeline) and then used for answering
without subsequent revisions based on feedback.

\paragraph{(3) Agentic unified models (UM-Ag).}
UM-Ag systems execute a bounded \emph{closed-loop} protocol that repeatedly plans, generates, re-perceives, and revises before answering.
Unlike Decoupled systems where $G$ is produced once, UM-Ag induces an answer by rolling out a trajectory of intermediate artifacts
$\{G_t\}_{t=0}^{K-1}$ and internal states $\{s_t\}_{t=0}^{K}$ under a matched budget:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
s_{t+1} &= \mathrm{Plan}(s_t, x, G_{<t}), \\
G_t &= \mathrm{Gen}(x, s_{t}), \\
x_{t+1} &= \mathrm{Perceive}(x, G_t) ,\\
\hat{y} &= \mathrm{Ans}(x, G_{\le K-1}),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:umag_loop}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Perceive}$ captures feedback channels such as re-reading the image with a new crop, verifying a generated explanation,
executing a tool to check a hypothesis, or updating memory/state. The key distinction is \emph{interactive refinement}:
later artifacts $G_{t+1}$ are conditioned on earlier artifacts and feedback, enabling correction and targeted information acquisition.
In our study, tool-driven visual exploration settings such as \emph{GPT-4o + image tool} and \emph{nano banana pro} instantiate this loop by
iteratively creating/editing auxiliary visuals and re-querying perception under a matched budget.
UM-Ag therefore unifies generation and understanding at the \emph{protocol level} (a control policy plus bounded interaction),
rather than solely at the parameter or module boundary.

\paragraph{Summary.}
Fig.~\ref{fig:umm-taxonomy} summarizes this taxonomy and highlights how the three classes differ in where unification occurs (shared parameters vs.\ modular orchestration) and how intermediate artifacts are generated and consumed (single-pass vs.\ closed-loop refinement). We evaluate representative models from all three classes in our experiments.


\subsection{Implicit vs.\ Explicit Generation-Augmented Understanding}
\label{sec:implicit_explicit}

Implicit vs.\ explicit G2U corresponds to whether the system produces external intermediate artifacts.
In the \emph{implicit} setting, the system answers directly as $\hat{y}=\mathrm{Ans}(x,\varnothing)$.
In the \emph{explicit} setting, it generates $G$ and re-consumes it, as in \eqref{eq:unified_factorization}
(or via an agentic rollout with multiple $G_t$).

\paragraph{Decomposing G2U gains.}
Define the best Generate-then-Answer  protocol for $M$ as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{Generate-then-Answer }}(M;\mathcal{D})
\triangleq
\max\Big\{
&\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{1shot}}(M;\mathcal{D}), \\
&\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{agentic}}(M;\mathcal{D})
\Big\},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:perf_stepwise_def}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{1shot}}$ instantiates \eqref{eq:unified_factorization} with a single $G$,
and $\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{agentic}}$ instantiates it with a closed-loop rollout.
We then decompose
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta_{\mathrm{implicit}}
&= \mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{direct}}(M_{UM};\mathcal{D})-\mathrm{Perf}(\mathcal{B};\mathcal{D}), \\
\Delta_{\mathrm{explicit}}
&= \mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{stepwise}}(M_{UM};\mathcal{D})-\mathrm{Perf}_{\mathrm{direct}}(M_{UM};\mathcal{D}).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:g2u_decomp}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Failure modes.}
Explicit generation can also hurt if intermediate artifacts are inaccurate or misaligned with the intended plan, thereby misleading
subsequent reasoning.

\subsection{A Canonical Instantiation: Single-Backbone Multimodal Language Models}
\label{sec:unified_single_backbone}

A common E2E instantiation is a single decoder-only language-model backbone that can condition on $V$ and emit either text tokens
or modality tokens, enabling interleaved ``understand $\leftrightarrow$ generate'' trajectories~\citep{UALM}.
Let $\Sigma_T$ be the text vocabulary and $\Sigma_V$ the discrete vocabulary of a modality codec.
The model defines a joint vocabulary $\Sigma=\Sigma_T\cup\Sigma_V$ and performs next-token prediction over mixed sequences:
\begin{equation}
p_\theta(z_{1:K}\mid p(x))
=\prod_{k=1}^{K} p_\theta(z_k\mid z_{<k},p(x)),
\qquad z_k\in\Sigma.
\label{eq:joint_vocab_ar}
\end{equation}
Emitting $z_k\in\Sigma_T$ corresponds to answering, while emitting $z_k\in\Sigma_V$ corresponds to generating an artifact $G$.

\section{The Setup of UniG2U}
\subsection{Dataset Construction and Task Taxonomy}

\begin{figure*}[tbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{task.pdf}
  \caption{Task taxonomy overview of UniG2U.}
  \label{fig:task-taxonomy}
\end{figure*}

UniG2U is designed as a diagnostic benchmark to study when and why generation benefits multimodal understanding. Rather than focusing on a single task family, we construct a diverse dataset covering a wide spectrum of perceptual and cognitive structures. In total, UniG2U contains approximately \textbf{3,000 samples}, organized into \textbf{7 high-level categories} and \textbf{30 fine-grained subcategories}. This section details the taxonomy design and the motivation behind each category.

\subsubsection{Design Principles}
The dataset taxonomy is guided by two core principles.  
First, categories should reflect \emph{distinct cognitive structures}  rather than superficial dataset sources.  
Second, categories should differ in the \emph{potential utility of intermediate generation}: some tasks naturally admit useful visual intermediates, while others are primarily perceptual and may not benefit from explicit generation. This structure enables systematic analysis of when generation is helpful, neutral, or harmful.



\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Real-world Applications (200 samples).} Visually grounded tasks resembling practical applications, such as attentional focusing and visual shortest-path reasoning (VSP). These tasks typically require identifying relevant regions, tracking spatial relations, or following visual constraints across complex scenes. Data are primarily sourced from VSP-style benchmarks and attentional reasoning datasets.

    \item \textbf{Mathematical Reasoning (200 samples).} Visual mathematics and geometry problems, including both 2D and 3D geometric reasoning. Subtasks include planar and solid geometry, curated from datasets such as Geometry3K and AuxSolidMath. These problems often benefit from structured intermediates (e.g., drawing auxiliary lines, reconstructing geometric structure), making them ideal for evaluating explicit generation-augmented understanding.

    \item \textbf{STEM Reasoning (200 samples).} Known-answer scientific reasoning grounded in diagrams, including physics and optics problems. \paragraph{Summary.}
By structuring the benchmark across seven categories with distinct reasoning characteristics, UniG2U enables fine-grained analysis of the relationship between generation and understanding. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:task-taxonomy}, the taxonomy spans both generation-friendly categories (e.g., spatial reasoning, puzzles) and more perception-heavy categories, allowing us to probe when intermediate visual generation is helpful, neutral, or harmful. Instead of merely reporting aggregate accuracy, this design allows us to investigate how the effectiveness of generation varies across cognitive regimes, providing deeper insight into the mechanisms of unified multimodal models.

\subsubsection{Task Selection and Coverage}
\label{sec:task-selection}


\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{task bing.pdf}
  \caption{Task selection and coverage in UniG2U.}
  \label{fig:task-selection-coverage}
\end{figure}
\label{sec:task-format}

UniG2U is constructed around a single guiding hypothesis: there exists a family of multimodal understanding tasks for which intermediate visual generation can serve as an effective reasoning aid. Such tasks are common in practice, including (i) \textbf{spatial reasoning} where models benefit from externalizing transformations or layouts, (ii) \textbf{chart \& table reasoning} where re-expression or simplification of plots can reduce cognitive load, (iii) \textbf{puzzles and games} where intermediate states can be visualized to support state tracking, and (iv) \textbf{STEM/geometry} problems where auxiliary constructions and diagram manipulation often enable correct solutions.

To ensure both \emph{comprehensive coverage} and \emph{strong relevance} to the G2U objective, we curate \textbf{3,000} instances across \textbf{7} high-level categories and \textbf{30} subtasks (Table~\ref{tab:dataset}). As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:task-selection-coverage}, our candidate pool is collected from diverse public sources (e.g., IllusionBench, MMSI-Bench, Visual Puzzles, Geometry3K, AuxSolidMath, Uni-MMMU, ChartQA, RealUnify, BabyVision, PhyX, and VSP-style benchmarks), from which we further \emph{select and filter} instances that best reflect settings where intermediate generation is likely to assist understanding.

In particular, the benchmark includes 200 samples each for Real-world Applications, Mathematical Reasoning, and STEM Reasoning; 537 samples for Puzzles and Games; 100 samples for Chart \& Table Reasoning; 500 samples for Spatial Intelligence; and 1,263 samples for Perception Reasoning. This distribution allows us to analyze generation benefits across cognitive regimes ranging from generation-friendly spatial manipulation to primarily perceptual recognition.


Each instance in UniG2U is organized around a \emph{question-answering} format with an image (or a small set of images) as the primary input. In addition to the final answer, we annotate (when applicable) auxiliary information to facilitate diagnostic evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Category / Subtask labels} for fine-grained analysis across the taxonomy.
    \item \textbf{Answer type} (e.g., multiple-choice, short text, numeric), which determines post-processing and matching rules.
    \item \textbf{Generation requirement tags} indicating whether a task naturally admits helpful intermediate visual representations (e.g., geometric construction, state transition visualization, chart re-expression).
\end{itemize}

Importantly, UniG2U does \emph{not} force a single canonical intermediate image for every problem. Instead, we treat intermediate generation as a \emph{reasoning tool} whose usefulness should be measured by whether it improves final task performance.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:eval-protocol}

Our evaluation protocol is designed to enable \emph{fair comparison} and to isolate the \emph{causal contribution of generation}.
\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{stepwise.pdf}
  \caption{Overview of our two evaluation prompt formats.}
  \label{fig:stepwise-protocol}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Unified evaluation environment.}
We run evaluations under a unified architecture and execution environment using \texttt{lmms-eval} whenever possible. Only when a model is not supported by this environment do we switch to the model's native evaluation stack, while keeping the rest of the protocol (prompt template, budget matching, and decoding settings) identical.

\paragraph{Unified prompting (Direct vs. Generate-then-Answer ).}
Following the dual-protocol design in RealUnify, we evaluate all models under two prompt formats: \textbf{Direct} and \textbf{Generate-then-Answer(GtA) }.
In \textbf{Direct} prompting, the model is asked to solve each instance end-to-end with a single unified prompt that includes: (i) task instruction, (ii) input image(s), (iii) question, and (iv) required answer format.
In \textbf{Generate-then-Answer } prompting, we explicitly decompose the instance into staged sub-prompts to diagnose the contribution of generation.
Concretely, for tasks where \emph{understanding enhances generation}, the model first produces a text solution/constraint resolution, and then uses it as the instruction for image generation (``understanding $\rightarrow$ generation'');
for tasks where \emph{generation enhances understanding}, the model first generates an intermediate image and then answers using this intermediate image as additional context (``generation $\rightarrow$ understanding'').

For most Generate-then-Answer  instances, we adopt a single-round interaction (generate once, then answer).
For inherently multi-step problems (e.g., jigsaw, maze), we use an iterative Generate-then-Answer  variant: if an instance requires $K$ steps, the model alternates between producing the step-$k$ intermediate visual artifact and the corresponding step-$k$ decision/answer, until reaching step $K$.

\paragraph{Budget matching and interaction constraints.}
To prevent performance gains from being confounded by additional compute, we enforce \emph{budget matching} across models. For all models and for each task category, we set the same maximum text generation budget. For input images, we use each model's default resolution. If out-of-memory occurs, we reduce the maximum token length and adjust the input resolution to the nearest supported resolution that the model can reliably support.

Most tasks in the Generate-then-Answer  setting allow at most \textbf{one} intermediate image per instance. For inherently multi-step tasks, we allow up to $K$ intermediate images (one per step).

\textbf{Rounds}: single-round by default. For iterative tasks, we allow up to $K$ rounds (step-$1$ to step-$K$), where each round follows the fixed pattern: \textit{generate step-$k$ image $\rightarrow$ produce step-$k$ decision/answer}.

\paragraph{Unified decoding settings.}
We standardize decoding across models whenever supported (greedy decoding with temperature $0$ and top-$p{=}1.0$). When a model does not expose the same decoding controls, we use the closest deterministic setting available and report the configuration.

\paragraph{Evaluation modes.}
For each model, we report results under the \textbf{Direct} and \textbf{Generate-then-Answer } prompting protocols (see Fig.~\ref{fig:stepwise-protocol}). \textbf{Direct} answers each instance with a single end-to-end prompt. \textbf{Generate-then-Answer } decomposes the process into stages (e.g., generating an intermediate image and then answering with it as additional context) to isolate the effect of intermediate generation. However, some models are unable to support the Generate-then-Answer  mode due to architectural or interface constraints. In such cases, we apply model-specific handling based on the underlying limitation; these cases and the corresponding treatments are described in detail in the model section. This paired design ensures that the comparison focuses on \emph{the effect of generation itself}, rather than differences in prompting, environment, compute, or tool availability.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\paragraph{Accuracy.}
We report accuracy (or exact match) as the primary metric. Depending on the task format, we compute correctness via (i) \emph{rule-based evaluation} using normalization followed by regex matching, or (ii) \emph{LLM-as-a-judge evaluation} by calling the GPT-4o API with task-specific grading prompts. Unless otherwise noted, GPT-4o is used to adjudicate non-trivial free-form outputs and visual-dependent judgments.

\paragraph{G2U Gain.}
Our core metric is \textbf{G2U Gain}, defined as the accuracy improvement relative to each model's corresponding \emph{base model}. We report two variants:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Direct\ G2U\ Gain} \;&=\; \mathrm{Acc}^{\text{Direct}}_{\text{Model}} \; -\; \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{Base}}, \label{eq:direct_g2u_gain}\\
\mathrm{GtA \ G2U\ Gain} \;&=\; \mathrm{Acc}^{\text{GtA}}_{\text{Model}} \; -\; \mathrm{Acc}_{\text{Base}}. \label{eq:stepwise_g2u_gain}
\end{align}
This definition isolates the net benefit introduced by the unified model beyond its underlying base model, separately under the \textbf{Direct} and \textbf{Generate-then-Answer } protocols. We compute G2U Gain both overall and per category/subtask to quantify when generation helps, is neutral, or harms understanding.

\paragraph{Generate-then-Answer  intermediate effectiveness: RA and AL.}
To assess the usefulness of intermediate images in the Generate-then-Answer  protocol, we further introduce two alignment metrics evaluated by GPT-4o with customized prompts for each task family.

\textbf{Reasoning-to-Visual Alignment (RA).}
RA measures whether the generated intermediate image aligns with the intended reasoning scaffold specified in the \texttt{generation\_prompt}. It aggregates instruction adherence, visual quality, and task relevance; the weighting scheme is provided in Fig.~\ref{fig:Rubric weights}. All RA scores are evaluated by GPT-4o under a standardized rubric.

\textbf{Answer-to-Visual Alignment (AL).}
AL measures whether the final answer is consistent with both the generated intermediate image and the original question. It aggregates visual--answer consistency, question--answer alignment, and reasoning coherence; the weighting scheme is provided in Fig.~\ref{fig:Rubric weights}. All AL scores are evaluated by GPT-4o under the same rubric.

\paragraph{Scoring schema.}
For both RA and AL, GPT-4o produces a final score on a 5-point scale by aggregating rubric-specific sub-scores according to the weights above. We report the mean RA/AL over all instances, and additionally provide breakdowns for each model and each subtask when applicable.

\subsection{Models and Implementation Details}
\label{sec:models-impl}



To study G2U under realistic model ecosystems, we evaluate three families of systems.

\paragraph{Base VLMs (understanding-only).}
We first benchmark strong vision-language models (VLMs) that perform \emph{pure understanding} without generating intermediate images. These base VLMs serve as the primary reference points for computing G2U Gain (\cref{eq:direct_g2u_gain,eq:stepwise_g2u_gain}).

\paragraph{Closed-source end-to-end unified models.}
We benchmark representative closed-source unified models that natively integrate multimodal understanding and image generation within a single model interface (i.e., the model can directly follow the \textbf{Direct} and \textbf{Generate-then-Answer } protocols without external tool wiring). These models serve as strong ``all-in-one'' baselines for understanding-through-generation.

\paragraph{Agentic unified models (LLM + image generator).}
Finally, we construct \emph{agentic} unified systems by composing a planner LLM with an external image generation tool and an optional re-perception step. Concretely, the planner first produces a \texttt{generation\_prompt}, calls the image generator to obtain an intermediate image, and then answers the original question conditioned on both the original inputs and the generated intermediate image.

We implement multiple agentic configurations to cover diverse tool ecosystems, including combinations such as \textbf{GPT-4o + GPT-image} and \textbf{Qwen + Qwen-Image-Edit}, as well as other analogous LLM$+$image-generator pairings (e.g., ``nano banana pro''). This family allows us to disentangle whether G2U gains arise from \emph{end-to-end unification} or from \emph{tool-using orchestration} that externalizes intermediate visual states.

\paragraph{Choosing base models when not explicitly provided.}
When a unified model does not have an official corresponding VLM base model (or its base model is not a vision-language model), we select the closest available VLM baseline in terms of training data and architecture, and use it as the base
model for comparison.

\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{sec:research-questions}

We define four research questions (RQs) to guide our study of understanding-through-generation (G2U) in unified multimodal models, and we answer them in the Experiments/Results section.

\paragraph{RQ1: Unified vs.\ base models.}
Do unified models exhibit consistent advantages over their corresponding base models on our G2U benchmark, under the protocols defined in \cref{sec:eval-protocol}?
\par\noindent\textbf{Experiment design:} For each unified model, we identify its corresponding base VLM and evaluate both under the same datasets, prompt templates, and inference budgets. We report absolute performance and G2U Gain overall and per category to characterize where unification yields benefits.

\paragraph{RQ2: Generate-then-Answer  vs.\ direct generation.}
For G2U tasks, do unified models benefit more from \emph{explicit} Generate-then-Answer  generation of intermediate auxiliary images, or can \emph{direct} generation achieve comparable understanding performance?
\par\noindent\textbf{Experiment design:} We run each unified model in both protocols (\textbf{Direct} vs.\ \textbf{Generate-then-Answer }; \cref{sec:eval-protocol}) under matched budgets, compare final-task performance, and quantify the delta between the two modes to isolate the contribution of explicit intermediate visualizations.

\paragraph{RQ3: Correlation within unified-model classes.}
Within the same class of unified models, do models show correlated performance patterns on G2U tasks, suggesting class-consistent strengths and weaknesses?
\par\noindent\textbf{Experiment design:} We group models by image-generation paradigm and unification design (e.g., AR/diffusion/flow/discrete; end-to-end vs.\ decoupled) following \cref{tab:unified-models}. We form model-by-task (or model-by-category) performance vectors and compute pairwise rank correlations (Spearman) within and across classes to test whether same-class models behave similarly.

\paragraph{RQ4: Intermediate-image instruction following and usefulness.}
When explicitly generating intermediate auxiliary images, do unified models reliably follow the given instructions and produce helpful, question-aligned visuals that genuinely support reasoning?
\par\noindent\textbf{Experiment design:} We evaluate Generate-then-Answer  intermediate outputs with the two rubric-based metrics defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:metrics}: \textbf{RA}  and \textbf{AL} . We then analyze how RA/AL relate to downstream task performance and to the GtA-vs-direct gains.

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} summarizes the performance of all evaluated models on UniG2U across seven task categories and the overall average.
All values are reported as accuracy (\%), and the rightmost column additionally reports $\Delta$, the relative performance difference with respect to the corresponding base model.However,not all unified models evaluated in this study support image editing or intermediate visual modification.
For certain models, the absence of a public editing interface or the lack of edit-related training data makes edit-style Generate-then-Answer generation effectively out-of-distribution (OOD).
Including their Generate-then-Answer results in the main comparison table would therefore introduce unfair bias, as these models are not architecturally or training-wise designed to perform edit-based intermediate reasoning.
To ensure fairness in cross-model comparison, we exclude the Generate-then-Answer results of such models from the main results table.
Instead, we design a specialized evaluation protocol in Appendix~B to analyze their Generate-then-Answer behavior under adapted or constrained settings, allowing us to examine their generation-to-understanding characteristics without penalizing missing architectural capabilities.

In addition, for unified models whose base backbone remains frozen during training, we do not report their Direct results in the main comparison table.
Under ideal conditions, a fully frozen backbone would imply that Direct performance should match that of the corresponding base VLM.
However, in practice, architectural modifications such as additional projection layers, modality tokenization differences, decoder routing strategies, or inference pipeline variations can introduce measurable deviations.
To avoid conflating such design-level discrepancies with genuine generation-to-understanding gains, we exclude the Direct comparison for frozen-backbone models from the main results.
A detailed analysis of these discrepancies and their empirical impact is provided in Appendix~\ref{Frozen_Backbones}.
%
Models are organized into blocks according to their underlying base model.
Each block therefore represents a set of unified models that share the same base vision--language model (VLM).
Within each block, the row marked with an asterisk ($^*$) denotes the base model itself, and its $\Delta$ is set to $0.00$ by definition.
For other models in the same block, $\Delta$ measures the \emph{Generation-to-Understanding (G2U) Gain}, defined as the difference between the unified model's performance and that of its base model under matched evaluation protocols and inference budgets.

When a unified model does not explicitly provide an official VLM base model, we determine its grouping based on architectural similarity, training data composition, and empirical performance on general multimodal benchmarks (e.g., MathVista).
The detailed classification criteria and model-to-base mapping are described in Appendix~J.
This design ensures capability-level comparability and avoids attributing gains to differences in scale or pretraining data.

\paragraph{Overall Trends from a Coupling Perspective.}
~\\
\textbf{Takeaway 1.1: On the majority of tasks, unified models perform worse than their base VL models.}

Across most blocks, unified models do not exhibit consistent overall improvements over their corresponding base VLMs.
Importantly, this observation holds even under the \emph{Direct} protocol, where no explicit intermediate image is generated.
This suggests that generation-to-understanding (G2U) effects are not solely determined by the quality of externally generated artifacts, but also by how generation and understanding are coupled within the unified architecture.

In the Direct setting, unified models differ from their base models primarily through joint multimodal training objectives or parameter sharing between understanding and generation.
The generally small or negative $\Delta$ values indicate that such parameter-level coupling does not automatically yield stronger reasoning ability.
In some cases, the added generative capacity may even introduce representational trade-offs, slightly degrading pure understanding performance.

\paragraph{Category-Level Patterns.}
~\\
\textbf{Takeaway 1.2: Unified models show distinct capability improvements on Spatial Intelligence and Visual Illusions.}



A finer-grained analysis reveals structured task-dependent behaviors.
Unified models tend to show clearer gains in \textbf{Spatial Intelligence} tasks.
These tasks involve transformation, state tracking, or geometric manipulation, where unified training may encourage internal representations that better capture spatial structure.
Here, generation--understanding coupling appears to regularize spatial reasoning rather than merely providing external visual aids.

In contrast, for \textbf{Mathematical Reasoning} tasks that require precise symbolic or geometric consistency, unified models often underperform their base VLMs.
This suggests that coupling generative objectives with fine-grained symbolic reasoning may introduce interference.
The generative component, optimized for plausible visual synthesis, does not necessarily align with the strict structural constraints required for mathematical correctness.

For \textbf{Perception Reasoning} tasks, performance differences are generally small or negative.
These tasks primarily require fine-grained recognition and discrimination.
The results suggest that current unified training does not significantly enhance perceptual representations beyond what strong base VLMs already provide.
In other words, generation--understanding coupling has not yet translated into more discriminative low-level visual features.

\paragraph{Implications.}
Overall, the results suggest that G2U is fundamentally a question of representation coupling rather than image synthesis quality.
Unified architectures benefit reasoning only when the generative and understanding pathways are coherently aligned.
Otherwise, added generative capacity may function as an auxiliary objective that does not directly translate into stronger multimodal reasoning.

To investigate whether explicit intermediate visual generation provides additional benefits over direct unified inference, we evaluate each unified model under both the Direct and Generate-then-Answer protocols with matched inference budgets (Table~3).

\textbf{Takeaway 2.1: The generation-before-answering paradigm degrades performance across most logic-intensive tasks.}

% , as the forced generation of intermediate images often introduces and propagates visual errors.

At the category level (Fig.~\ref{fig:rq2_scatter}), a majority of points lie below the $y=x$ line, indicating that Generate-then-Answer inference does not consistently outperform Direct inference across task regimes. This trend is further reflected in the aggregate comparison (Fig.~\ref{fig:rq2_overall_bar}), where Generate-then-Answer accuracy is frequently lower than Direct for models supporting both protocols. Overall, Generate-then-Answer tends to underperform relative to Direct in most settings.

For several unified models (e.g., OneCAT-3B, UAE, Ovis-U1, and MIO), the degradation is substantial. This observation suggests that explicitly externalizing intermediate visuals does not automatically improve reasoning performance. Instead, Generate-then-Answer prompting can introduce additional failure modes when the generated artifact is inaccurate, semantically misaligned, incomplete, or poorly integrated into the subsequent answering stage.

Importantly, this overall decline should not be interpreted as evidence that generation itself is harmful. From a coupling perspective, Direct inference already enables end-to-end interaction between generation and understanding within a shared parameter space. In many end-to-end unified models, generation capability is implicitly embedded in the backbone and shapes internal representations even when no explicit image is produced. As a result, Direct mode benefits from generation-trained representations while avoiding the risk of committing to a concrete but imperfect intermediate artifact. In contrast, Generate-then-Answer mode forces the model to externalize a visual hypothesis before answering. When instruction-following fidelity or generative precision is limited, this explicit commitment can constrain downstream reasoning and propagate errors.

A qualitative analysis of representative cases (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_failure_cases}) reveals that performance drops are often associated with poorly constructed intermediate visuals—for example, auxiliary diagrams that omit critical constraints, inaccurate geometric constructions, distorted spatial layouts, or cluttered edits that obscure discriminative cues. In such scenarios, the generated image becomes a source of noise rather than a reasoning aid, leading to error amplification in the final answer.

\textbf{Takeaway 2.2: Generating intermediate images provides noticeable performance gains over direct output in transformation-intensive scenarios.}

% where intermediate states can be explicitly grounded and updated.

Nevertheless, Generate-then-Answer generation is not uniformly detrimental. 
A more fine-grained subtask analysis reveals that Generate-then-Answer inference yields 
consistent improvements in several transformation-intensive regimes. 
In particular, on \textit{maze}, \textit{jigsaw}, \textit{sliding}, and \textit{MSR}, 
Generate-then-Answer outperforms Direct for a substantial fraction of models 
(see Table~\ref{tab:rq2_spatial_subtasks}). 
These tasks share a common structural property: 
they require multi-step state transitions, spatial manipulation, 
or iterative hypothesis refinement, where intermediate visual 
externalization can meaningfully support reasoning.

For example, maze solving involves tracking a trajectory through constrained spatial paths; 
sliding puzzles require maintaining and updating object configurations across discrete transformation steps; 
and MSR-style problems typically demand explicit modeling of spatial relations or multi-step geometric transformations. 
Crucially, these tasks share a common property: they require \emph{sequential, multi-step reasoning}, 
where intermediate states must be explicitly constructed, verified, and updated over time.

In such scenarios, Generate-then-Answer generation plays a role analogous to visual reasoning. 
By externalizing intermediate visual hypotheses, the model decomposes a complex transformation into a sequence of structured sub-steps, 
rather than implicitly compressing the entire reasoning trajectory into a single forward pass. 
The generated intermediate artifact effectively serves as an external workspace, 
stabilizing state tracking, reducing implicit memory burden, and constraining the solution space at each step. 
This explicit decomposition mitigates error accumulation in long transformation chains and can improve decision consistency.

Importantly, these gains are not universal but concentrated in tasks whose reasoning structure is intrinsically visual, 
sequential, and transformation-heavy. 
This pattern suggests that explicit generation is beneficial when it functions as a structured reasoning scaffold— 
analogous to textual CoT—by making intermediate spatial states observable and manipulable, 
rather than requiring them to be implicitly maintained within hidden representations.

Taken together, these findings indicate that the decisive factor is not merely whether generation is performed explicitly, but how reliably intermediate artifacts are produced and how tightly generation and understanding are coupled within the architecture. Direct unified inference already captures a substantial portion of generation-induced representational benefits, whereas explicit Generate-then-Answer generation provides gains only when intermediate visuals are structurally accurate and semantically aligned with the reasoning objective.

While RQ1 and RQ2 examine absolute performance and protocol differences, RQ3 asks whether \emph{G2U gains exhibit structured correlation patterns}.

\textbf{Takeaway 3: Correlations among tasks and models are highly significant: tasks with similar cognitive demands, as well as models sharing identical base weights, consistently exhibit parallel performance trends.}

Instead of raw accuracy, we analyze the relative offset $\Delta$ (unified model minus its corresponding base model), which isolates the generation-induced contribution and reduces confounding factors from backbone strength.

\textbf{Task-level correlation.}
We compute pairwise Spearman correlations between subtasks by treating each subtask as a vector of $\Delta$ values across models.
The resulting heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:rq3_task_corr}) reveals that G2U gains are not randomly distributed across tasks.
Within logic-intensive reasoning regimes, subtasks often show positive correlation—for example, jigsaw and maze exhibit similar gain patterns—suggesting that models that benefit from generation on one structured reasoning subtask tend to benefit on related ones as well. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
In contrast, logic-oriented subtasks frequently exhibit negative correlation with recognition-oriented subtasks, indicating a trade-off: models that gain from generation on structured reasoning may not gain (and can even regress) on fine-grained recognition.
Interestingly, recognition subtasks are not uniformly aligned with each other: some pairs are positively correlated, while others are negatively correlated (e.g., shape vs.\ scene), implying that ``recognition'' is itself heterogeneous and sensitive to what visual cues intermediate generation emphasizes. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

\textbf{Model-level correlation.}
We also compute Spearman correlations between models by treating each model as a vector of $\Delta$ across subtasks.
The model-level heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:rq3_model_corr_s3}, Fig.~\ref{fig:rq3_model_corr_s5}) shows clear grouping effects.
Models sharing the same base model exhibit the strongest correlation in their $\Delta$ patterns, indicating that base representations substantially determine how generation-induced capacity transfers to understanding. 
Architecturally similar models also show correlated gain signatures, but the effect is generally weaker than base-model sharing, suggesting that architecture alone does not fully determine G2U behavior without a shared representational starting point. 

Overall, these results suggest that G2U gains are jointly shaped by (i) task structure (logic vs.\ recognition and their subtypes) and (ii) representational coupling inherited from the base model, with architectural similarity providing an additional but secondary source of consistency.

Two consistent patterns emerge. 
First, recognition-oriented tasks (e.g., Perception Reasoning and certain real-world scenarios) generally achieve higher alignment scores, 
indicating that models can reliably generate visually faithful intermediate artifacts when transformations are relatively direct. 
In contrast, logic-intensive and structurally constrained tasks—such as geometric construction, symbolic reasoning, or multi-step state tracking—tend to exhibit lower alignment scores, 
reflecting the difficulty of faithfully externalizing complex reasoning structures.

Second, alignment quality strongly correlates with Generate-then-Answer effectiveness (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}). 
Tasks and models with higher IR/AL scores typically demonstrate larger gains under Generate-then-Answer relative to Direct inference, 
whereas low-alignment settings often lead to performance degradation due to error propagation from misaligned intermediate artifacts.

These findings suggest that the effectiveness of explicit generation is governed by alignment fidelity rather than generative capacity alone: 
intermediate images are beneficial only when they are both instruction-consistent and reasoning-aligned.


\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

In this work, we introduce \textbf{UniG2U}, a large-scale diagnostic benchmark for systematically studying when and how generation helps understanding in unified multimodal models. Across 3,000 curated instances spanning seven cognitive regimes, we show that generation-to-understanding (G2U) gains are highly task-dependent and architecture-dependent. Unified models do not consistently outperform their base VLMs; instead, improvements emerge primarily in spatial transformation and state-tracking tasks, where generation-trained representations regularize structural reasoning. Moreover, explicit Generate-then-Answer generation does not universally surpass direct unified inference. While intermediate visual externalization can assist reasoning, it often introduces new failure modes when generated artifacts are inaccurate or misaligned. Our correlation analyses further reveal that G2U gains follow structured task- and model-level patterns shaped by representational priors inherited from base models.

Looking forward, advancing G2U requires stronger integration mechanisms between generation and reasoning. Future directions include representation-level alignment objectives, reliability-aware or self-verifying intermediate generation, and closed-loop agentic refinement that mitigates error propagation. Extending beyond image-only intermediates to structured or multimodal reasoning artifacts may further enhance robustness. Additionally, investigating scaling laws of G2U effects and designing causal evaluation protocols to isolate representation changes remain important open problems. We hope UniG2U provides a principled foundation for future research toward unified multimodal systems in which generation and understanding mutually reinforce rather than interfere with one another.

\subsection{Maze Solving Task}

\subsubsection{Task Description}

\textbf{Input:} Maze image with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Black squares: walls (impassable)
    \item White squares: paths (walkable)
    \item Blue dot: starting position
    \item Green frame: goal region
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Task:} Find a sequence of moves from start to goal.

\textbf{Constraints:} Legal moves: up, down, left, right; one cell per step; no diagonals; no wall crossing.

\textbf{Output:} \verb|<ANSWER_JSON>["right", "down", ...]|

\textbf{Metrics:} \texttt{maze\_text\_exact}, \texttt{maze\_text\_frame\_acc}

\section{RA and AL Evaluation Pipeline and Full Prompt Specification}
\label{app:ra-al}

\subsection{Purpose}
This appendix documents the complete evaluation protocol used for Visual Chain-of-Thought (Visual CoT) quality assessment with two judge metrics:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RA} (Reasoning-to-Visual Alignment)
\item \textbf{AL} (Answer-to-Visual Alignment)
\end{itemize}
The implementation is fully prompt-based and uses a multimodal LLM judge.

\subsection{Per-sample Inputs and Metadata}
For each sample \(i\), the evaluator reads:
\begin{itemize}
\item Original image(s): \(I_{\text{orig}}^{(i)}\)
\item Generated auxiliary image(s): \(I_{\text{aux}}^{(i)}\)
\item Generation prompt: \(p_{\text{gen}}^{(i)}\)
\item Original question: \(q^{(i)}\)
\item Final Stage-2 answer: \(a^{(i)}\)
\item Task name: \(t^{(i)}\)
\end{itemize}

The metadata file contains:
\begin{verbatim}
{
  "doc_id": ...,
  "task": ...,
  "generation_prompt": ...,
  "generated_images": [...],
  "question": ...,
  "stage2_answer": ...
}
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Metric Definitions}
\paragraph{RA.}
\[
\mathrm{RA}_i = \mathcal{J}_{\mathrm{RA}}\!\left(I_{\text{orig}}^{(i)}, I_{\text{aux}}^{(i)},
p_{\text{gen}}^{(i)}, q^{(i)}, t^{(i)}\right)\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}
\]
RA evaluates whether generated auxiliary visualization follows the generation instruction and is useful/relevant.

\paragraph{AL.}
\[
\mathrm{AL}_i = \mathcal{J}_{\mathrm{AL}}\!\left(I_{\text{orig}}^{(i)}, I_{\text{aux}}^{(i)},
q^{(i)}, a^{(i)}, t^{(i)}\right)\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}
\]
AL evaluates whether the final answer is consistent with visual evidence and question intent.

\paragraph{Dataset-level averages.}
For \(N\) samples:
\[
\overline{\mathrm{RA}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathrm{RA}_i,\qquad
\overline{\mathrm{AL}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathrm{AL}_i.
\]

\subsection{End-to-end Evaluation Flow}
For each sample:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Load metadata JSON.
\item Resolve \texttt{generated\_images} (relative paths are optionally resolved via \texttt{base\_dir}).
\item Construct RA prompt: \texttt{Base RA Prompt + Category-specific RA Addition}.
\item Call multimodal judge with images \([I_{\text{orig}}, I_{\text{aux}}]\), parse \texttt{reasoning\_visual\_score}.
\item Construct AL prompt: \texttt{Base AL Prompt + Category-specific AL Addition}.
\item Call multimodal judge with images \([I_{\text{orig}}, I_{\text{aux}}]\), parse \texttt{answer\_visual\_score}.
\item Save output fields:
\texttt{ra\_score}, \texttt{ra\_reasoning}, \texttt{al\_score}, \texttt{al\_reasoning}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Judge Call Settings}
\begin{itemize}
\item Multimodal judge: GPT-4o compatible endpoint (Azure OpenAI interface).
\item Temperature: \(0.0\).
\item Max response tokens: \(3000\).
\item Retry strategy: metric-level retries and API-level retries with backoff.
\item Image encoding: JPEG base64 payload.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prompt Composition Rule}
Prompt construction uses this priority:
\begin{enumerate}
\item If \texttt{task\_category} is explicitly set, append that category's criteria.
\item Else, apply legacy task-name mapping:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{illusionbench} \(\rightarrow\) \texttt{perception}
\item \texttt{chartqa} \(\rightarrow\) \texttt{chart\_table}
\item \texttt{mathvista} \(\rightarrow\) \texttt{mathematical}
\end{itemize}
\item Else, use base prompt only.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Complete Base Prompt: RA}
\begin{verbatim}
You are a professional AI evaluation specialist for Visual Chain-of-Thought tasks.

## Task Context
- **Task**: {task}
- **Original Question**: {question}

## Your Objective
Evaluate if the **generated visualization images** correctly follow the
**generation prompt**.

## Images Provided
- **Image 1**: Original question image (the input)
- **Image 2+**: Generated visualization image(s) (the auxiliary output created
  to help answer the question)

## Generation Prompt (What model was instructed to generate)
"{generation_prompt}"

## Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Instruction Following (40%)
- Does the generated image follow the generation prompt instructions?
- Are all requested visual elements present?
- Does it match the specified visualization type?

### 2. Visual Quality (30%)
- Is the generated image clear and well-formed?
- Are visual elements properly rendered?
- Is it helpful as an auxiliary visualization?

### 3. Relevance to Task (30%)
- Does the visualization help answer the original question?
- Is it relevant to the task context?
- Does it provide useful visual cues?

## Scoring Scale (1-5)

- **5 Perfect**: Generated image(s) perfectly match generation prompt;
  all instructions followed; high quality; highly relevant
- **4 Good**: Generated image(s) mostly match prompt (80-90%);
  minor issues; good quality; relevant
- **3 Adequate**: Generated image(s) partially match prompt (60-70%);
  noticeable issues; acceptable quality
- **2 Poor**: Generated image(s) barely match prompt (30-50%);
  major issues; poor quality or relevance
- **1 Failed**: Generated image(s) completely fail to match prompt;
  unusable; irrelevant

## Important Notes
- Focus on whether the **generation prompt** was correctly executed
- Do NOT evaluate if the final answer is correct (that's a separate metric)
- Evaluate the **visualization quality** and **instruction adherence**

## Output Format
{
  "reasoning_visual_score": X,
  "reasoning": "Detailed evaluation of how well generated images match the
  generation prompt"
}
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Complete Base Prompt: AL}
\begin{verbatim}
You are a professional AI evaluation specialist for Visual Chain-of-Thought tasks.

## Task Context
- **Task**: {task}
- **Original Question**: {question}

## Your Objective
Evaluate if the **final answer** is consistent with the **generated visualization
images** and the **original question**.

## Images Provided
- **Image 1**: Original question image (the input)
- **Image 2+**: Generated visualization image(s) (auxiliary output used to
  derive the answer)

## Final Answer
"{answer}"

## Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Visual-Answer Consistency (50%)
- Is the answer supported by visual evidence in generated images?
- Can you trace the answer back to visual elements?
- Are there contradictions between answer and visuals?

### 2. Question-Answer Alignment (30%)
- Does the answer correctly address the original question?
- Is the answer format appropriate?
- Is it a valid response to the question?

### 3. Reasoning Coherence (20%)
- Does the reasoning flow make sense (original → generated visual → answer)?
- Is the answer logically derived from the visualization?
- Is the overall chain-of-thought coherent?

## Scoring Scale (1-5)

- **5 Perfect**: Answer perfectly matches generated visuals and question;
  fully consistent; logically sound; correct reasoning
- **4 Good**: Answer mostly consistent (80-90%);
  minor inconsistencies; generally correct reasoning
- **3 Adequate**: Answer partially consistent (60-70%);
  some contradictions; reasoning has gaps
- **2 Poor**: Answer barely consistent (30-50%);
  major contradictions; flawed reasoning
- **1 Failed**: Answer completely inconsistent with visuals or question;
  contradictory; nonsensical

## Important Notes
- Focus on **consistency** between answer and generated visuals
- Check if answer logically follows from the visualization
- Verify answer addresses the original question
- Do NOT evaluate if generation prompt was followed (that's RA metric)

## Output Format
{
  "answer_visual_score": X,
  "reasoning": "Detailed evaluation of answer consistency with visuals and question"
}
\end{verbatim}

Third, inconsistencies in the software environment, such as different versions of PyTorch, CUDA, or the transformers library, often introduce deterministic execution variances. Furthermore, differences in visual pre-processing pipelines, particularly image encoding formats (e.g., reading raw images via PIL versus applying JPEG compression), affect pixel-level inputs. Finally, operating in different numerical precisions can cause compounding errors. For instance, computing Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) in bfloat16 instead of float32 can accumulate numerical errors over long sequences, eventually shifting the output logits and altering the results.

As discussed in Section~5, not all unified models evaluated in this work are trained with explicit image-to-image (edit) capabilities. For such models, Generate-then-Answer generation that requires intermediate image editing inherently falls outside their training distribution, effectively constituting an out-of-distribution (OOD) setting. Including their Generate-then-Answer results directly in the main comparison table could therefore introduce an unfair bias.

To better understand the effect of this OOD intermediate generation, we conduct additional ablation studies on a representative subset of edit-untrained models, with the results summarized in Table~\ref{tab:sheet7_scaled}. Specifically, we categorize their Generate-then-Answer evaluation into two alternative variants:

\paragraph{(1) OOD-Image Generation (UAE and X-Omni).}
Despite lacking edit training, the first two models are forced to generate an intermediate image under the standard Generate-then-Answer protocol (i.e., conditioned on the original input image). The generated image is then re-consumed for answering. This setting measures how severely forcing OOD intermediate generation degrades downstream reasoning performance.

\paragraph{(2) Prompt-Only Intermediate Generation (TokenFlow-XL).}
Instead of feeding the original input image into the intermediate generation stage, we restrict the final model to producing an auxiliary image conditioned strictly on the textual generation prompt (i.e., no image-to-image conditioning). The generated image is subsequently used as additional context for answering. This setting isolates the effect of prompt-level generation without invoking explicit edit-style image transformations.

\paragraph{Findings.}
As demonstrated in Table~\ref{tab:sheet7_scaled}, both Generate-then-Answer variants consistently lead to overall performance degradation compared to their Direct inference counterparts. Specifically, forcing OOD-Image generation causes overall performance drops of 2.33\% and 10.99\% for UAE and X-Omni, respectively, while relying on Prompt-Only generation leads to a 4.65\% drop for TokenFlow-XL. 

Furthermore, the qualitative examples presented in Figure~\ref{fig:irr_chart_know_quad11} corroborate these quantitative declines. Visual inspection reveals that the intermediate images produced by these edit-untrained models are often completely chaotic, structurally irrelevant, or fail to encode the necessary information. Instead of aiding the reasoning process, these misaligned generations actively mislead the subsequent answering stage.

Overall, these ablation results validate our decision to exclude the Generate-then-Answer scores of edit-untrained models from the main comparison table. They strongly emphasize that Generate-to-Understand (G2U) gains rely critically on explicit and training-aligned intermediate generation capabilities.

\section{Failure Case Taxonomy for Generation-Augmented Understanding}
\label{sec:appendix_failure_cases}

In this appendix, we present representative failure cases of generation-augmented reasoning.
We organize all examples under a unified three-way taxonomy based on the relationship
between generated diagrams and downstream reasoning performance.

\paragraph{Why failure cases matter.}
Generation-augmented understanding is often motivated by the intuition that ``drawing helps thinking'':
a faithful diagram can externalize latent structure, reduce working-memory burden, and provide
geometric/physical constraints that guide computation.
However, \emph{visual generation is itself a structured prediction problem}.
When the generated artifact is even slightly inconsistent with the underlying task, the downstream solver
may inherit or amplify those errors (e.g., by trusting a distorted geometry, an incorrect scale, or a spurious path).
Thus, it is important to characterize \emph{how} generation fails, not merely \emph{whether} an answer is wrong.

\paragraph{Selection protocol.}
We curate cases from diverse domains (geometry, physics, charts, and factual QA) to ensure the taxonomy
covers (i) tasks where diagrams are essential (geometry/physics),
(ii) tasks where diagrams can be helpful but must encode precise structure (charts),
and (iii) tasks where diagrams are unnecessary (pure textual knowledge).
For each case, we pair the original prompt with a model-generated visual output and
highlight the key mismatch that explains downstream failure.

\paragraph{Three criteria: validity, relevance, and utility.}
Across domains, successful generation must satisfy three requirements:
(i) \textbf{structural validity} (the diagram is internally consistent and respects physical/geometric rules),
(ii) \textbf{semantic relevance} (the content corresponds to the task entities and relations),
and (iii) \textbf{reasoning utility} (the diagram adds constraints or reduces ambiguity in a way that supports solving).
Our taxonomy maps naturally to violations of these criteria: Category~I breaks validity,
Category~II satisfies relevance but lacks utility, and Category~III fails relevance altogether.

\newcommand{\tightcaption}[1]{\caption{\vspace{-2pt}#1\vspace{-6pt}}}

\paragraph{Category I: Capability Failure.}
The model fails to generate structurally correct or quantitatively valid diagrams.
Rendered visuals are geometrically distorted, structurally inconsistent, or physically invalid,
making downstream reasoning unreliable or impossible.
This category is the most direct failure mode: even if the image appears ``diagram-like'',
it violates invariants required by the task.

\smallnote{\textbf{Common symptoms.}}
\vspace{-2pt}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
\item \emph{Geometric inconsistency:} angles/parallel lines/circle tangency implied by the prompt are not preserved.
\item \emph{Quantitative distortion:} lengths, slopes, or axis scales are visually inconsistent with stated values.
\item \emph{Physical invalidity:} forces/velocities are drawn with wrong directions or incompatible magnitudes.
\item \emph{Missing constraints:} key elements (labels, right-angle marks, reference points) are omitted or misplaced.
\end{itemize}

\smallnote{\textbf{Why it breaks reasoning.}}
Even a strong textual solver may implicitly treat the diagram as ground truth.
When the generated visual is invalid, the solver can be pushed toward incorrect intermediate computations
(e.g., wrong triangle similarity, wrong area under a curve, wrong cross-product direction), or may be forced to ignore
the diagram entirely, losing the intended benefit of externalization.

\smallnote{\textbf{Implication.}}
Category~I indicates a bottleneck in the generator's \emph{diagram grammar}:
models may learn surface styles (lines, arrows, labels) without learning domain invariants.
A practical mitigation is to introduce lightweight validation or self-checking
(e.g., verify right angles, axis monotonicity, connectivity constraints) before passing the diagram to the solver,
or to route such instances back to a pure-text solution when validity is uncertain.

\paragraph{Category II: Surface-Relevant but Non-Utility Generation.}
The model generates diagrams that are semantically related to the prompt,
yet they add no constraints or computational benefit for solving.
In these cases, the generation is ``on-topic'' but \emph{not instrumental}:
the diagram either restates what is already explicit in text, or fails to encode the specific structure needed.

\smallnote{\textbf{Common symptoms.}}
\vspace{-2pt}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
\item \emph{Correct but redundant:} the diagram matches the description but does not reduce uncertainty.
\item \emph{Missing operational structure:} key relations for computation (e.g., graph scales, maze connectivity) are absent.
\item \emph{Aesthetic completion:} the model fills the page with plausible visuals rather than task-sufficient constraints.
\end{itemize}

\smallnote{\textbf{Why it fails to help.}}
Utility is not guaranteed by relevance: even a perfectly relevant illustration can be useless if it does not support the
specific operation needed (e.g., reading off a slope, tracing a path, or identifying a computable sub-structure).
This failure mode is particularly important because it can \emph{waste} generation budget without improving accuracy,
and may even distract downstream reasoning by introducing extra, non-actionable details.

\paragraph{Category III: Irrelevant Generation.}
The generated image is not meaningfully related to the task, indicating complete
generation--task misalignment.
Here, generation is triggered when it should not be (e.g., factual QA),
or the model produces a visual that does not encode the required structured content (e.g., ChartQA).

\smallnote{\textbf{Common symptoms.}}
\vspace{-2pt}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
\item \emph{Mode confusion:} the model treats a purely textual query as an invitation to draw.
\item \emph{Structure loss:} the output resembles an image but lacks the schema needed (bars/axes/labels) to be a chart.
\item \emph{Hallucinated content:} visuals introduce unrelated entities, styles, or scenes not grounded in the prompt.
\end{itemize}

\smallnote{\textbf{Why it hurts.}}
Irrelevant generation is harmful beyond wasted compute: it can shift the solver's attention to spurious cues,
reduce trust in the system, and complicate debugging (the output looks ``confident'' but is unrelated).
In knowledge QA, the correct behavior is often to answer directly; in chart reasoning, the correct behavior is to
preserve structured data rather than produce decorative images.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
Across geometry, physics, chart reasoning, and factual QA, these examples show that generation alone does not guarantee improved understanding.
For generation to help reasoning, it must simultaneously satisfy structural correctness, semantic relevance, and reasoning utility;
failure along any dimension can degrade performance.

\smallnote{\textbf{Practical lesson for G2U system design.}}
\vspace{-2pt}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
\item \textbf{Validate} when diagrams are intended to be constraints (Category~I risk).
\item \textbf{Predict utility} and skip redundant illustrations (Category~II risk).
\item \textbf{Route by modality} and avoid image generation for pure text tasks (Category~III risk).
\end{enumerate}
